Advancing General-Purpose Reasoning Models with
Modular Gradient Surgery

Abstract

Reinforcement learning (RL) has emerged as a powerful tool for strengthening large language model (LLM) reasoning in various domains. However, combining heterogeneity for training a general-purpose reasoning model remains challenging. We evaluate two common approaches, Sequential RL and Mixed RL, across math reasoning, open-ended chat, and instruction following. We find that both suffer from cross-domain interference that degrades overall performance. To address this, we introduce Modular Gradient Surgery (MGS), a method that detects and resolves conflicting gradients at a modular level within the transformer. Applied to Llama and Qwen models, MGS achieves 4.3 and 4.5 point average improvements over multi-task RL, substantially expanding model capability.

Figure 1: Effectiveness Comparison

Comparison of Sequential RL, Mixed RL, and Modular Gradient Surgery (MGS). MGS achieves the best multi-domain performance frontier.

The Challenge: Mode Interference

When training models on diverse domains (e.g., rigid math rewards vs. flexible chat rewards), standard techniques fail in two specific ways:

Forgetting

Performance degradation on previously trained domains during Sequential RL. Optimizing for Math often erases Chat capabilities.

Reasoning Rigidity

Training on one domain can constrain performance gains on subsequent ones. Math training often leads to a "rigid" model that struggles to explore in Chat.

Modular Gradient Surgery (MGS)

MGS identifies that gradient conflicts are not uniform. Instead of projecting gradients globally, we resolve interference at the module level (MLP, Attention blocks). This allows the model to preserve compatible updates while selectively mitigating destructive interference.

Figure 2: MGS Architecture

Visualizing conflicting task gradients being projected and aggregated independently within MLP and Attention modules.

Key Results

  • State-of-the-Art Performance: MGS consistently achieves the highest average score across Math, Chat, and IF benchmarks.
  • Balanced Capabilities: Unlike sequential training, MGS prevents "catastrophic forgetting" and maintains strong performance in all domains simultaneously.
  • Scalability: MGS generalizes to prolonged training and more complex task combinations with minimal overhead.
